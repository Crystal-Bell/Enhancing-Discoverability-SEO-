### Enhancing Discoverability (SEO)
Since you want your work to be found by the right people, focusing on "surgical" (precise) terminology is exactly the right instinct. Here is how to apply that to your repositories:
 * **Keyword-Rich Descriptions**: Update the "About" section for each repository to include specific, descriptive keywords that mirror your system's actual functions. Keep these descriptions concise—ideally 5 to 15 words—starting with your primary keyword.
 * **Strategic Topics (Tags)**: Use GitHub’s "Topics" feature to categorize your work. Think about the terms someone would actually search for to find your *Transdisciplinary OS* or *M.A.D. WORKS* components—like "bio-synchronized," "metacognition," or "circular-economy"—and apply these as tags to your repositories.
 * **The README as a Landing Page**: Treat your README.md as the primary entrance to your work. Ensure it explains the *purpose* and *utility* of the project clearly. Using consistent, keyword-rich headings and semantic markdown will help search engines and GitHub’s internal search index your work more accurately.
 * **Internal Backlinking**: If your repositories are linked (as you've done with your "supplemented by" strategy), explicitly link between related issues, discussions, and repositories within your documentation.
### Adding and Managing Files
You can add files to your repositories directly through the GitHub interface whenever you are ready:
 * **Upload via Interface**: Navigate to your main repository page, click the **"Add file"** dropdown menu, and select **"Upload files"**.
 * **Drag-and-Drop**: You can simply drag and drop your files or folders directly into the browser window, then commit those changes to your branch.
 * **Commit Strategy**: By default, you can commit directly to your main branch for quick updates. However, if you are experimenting with new code or features, it is standard practice to create a new branch, test your changes, and then merge them back when you are satisfied.
